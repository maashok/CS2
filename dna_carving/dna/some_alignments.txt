The H3N2 virus is a variant of a virus found in pigs. It has been
found to host in various animals, as shown in the above DNA sequences.

This difference suggests that the strains of the virus are more similar
when they are infecting the same species of animal, and are collected
around the same time. Since viruses are very quick at mutating, in the 4
years between the collection of the virus DNA sequence from the
dog and the duck, the virus would have mutated a lot, leading to not that
great of an alignment between the two. In addition, the fact that
the virus would have to be different to affect dogs vs. ducks, which
are quite different organisms, leads to the -299 alignment score
for the two sequences.

However, the alignment score for two DNA sequences of the H3N2 virus
in the duck host, collected just 2 days apart, is a 6, which is
much higher. As discussed before, this is because of the virus not
having much time to mutate, and the only difference between the two
hosts being small differences due to different genes being passed
down from parents. Otherwise, they are of the same species, and inherently
very similar.

This comparison helps us note the fact that DNA alignment techniques
can be used to figure out how similar different organisms are.
